## Root family law

Family routing is the first real organizing judgment Aurora makes after context interpretation.
A family is not a label for chart cosmetics.
It is a structural engine with habitat, anti-habitat, competitors, stage profile, invalidation pressure, and downstream geometry implications.

## Family competition law

A wrapper-only GPT must:
- generate candidates from actual family doctrine, not generic market stereotypes
- keep weak-but-plausible families alive when elimination is not yet earned
- preserve competitors explicitly
- allow contested primaries, multiple live families, deferred classification, and no-valid-family outcomes
- never use hidden numeric confidence to replace doctrine

Competition dimensions the wrapper must remember:
- habitat fit
- anti-habitat conflict
- state alignment
- regime compatibility
- contradiction load
- dependence on missing surfaces
- evidence not yet present
- deployability compatibility modifier
- competitor pressure

- case identity and lineage refs

- candidate family block
- rejected family block with reasons
- surviving family block
- what would change the result

## Family ecology C-01 through C-05

### C-01 — Trend Continuation
**Canonical idea:** directional authority is already established and persists.
**Native habitat:** clean continuation states, accepted directional control, supportive regime, and persistence rather than rediscovery.
**Anti-habitat:** weak chop, failed follow-through, fake breakout states, or rotational balance dominating.
**Main competitors:** C-02 when the move is really a pullback-restoration story; C-03 when the supposed continuation is actually fresh release; C-04 when continuation authority is failing; C-05 when the move is inside balance rather than migration.
**Main anti-confusions:** stale trend labels, exhausted extension treated as healthy continuation, and symbol-bound “uptrend/downtrend” storytelling.
**Typical pattern children:** G1 accepted break-and-hold, sometimes G2 orderly continuation pullback.
**Stage posture:** strong in confirmed/continuation; weak when late or exhausted unless structure still clearly supports continuation.
**Bucket/regime note:** stronger where directional migration and continuation acceptance are genuinely visible, not merely imagined.

### C-02 — Trend Pullback / Pullback Continuation
**Canonical idea:** an existing directional engine remains valid while local retracement stays subordinate and constructive.
**Native habitat:** already-established directional authority with orderly retracement and restoration potential.
**Anti-habitat:** deterioration mistaken for pullback, fresh release mistaken for restoration, or balance reform dominating.
**Main competitors:** C-01 if no real pullback is present; C-04 if the retracement is actually failure/reversal; C-05 if balance logic is reforming; C-03 if compression-release rather than restoration explains the move better.
**Main anti-confusions:** any dip-in-trend ≠ pullback-continuation; orderly restoration matters more than loose directional bias.
**Typical pattern children:** G2 orderly continuation pullback.
**Stage posture:** strong in developing/continuation/re-entry, weaker when retracement becomes too late or structurally decayed.
**Bucket/regime note:** especially sensitive to execution continuity and hostile-environment burden because structurally valid pullbacks can still become unusable.

### C-03 — Breakout / Compression Release
**Canonical idea:** meaningful containment or compression resolves into real release and acceptance.
**Native habitat:** real pressure build-up, boundary importance, and evidence that expansion is more than noise.
**Anti-habitat:** random volatility spikes, local overextension with no compression parent, or failed release already taking over.
**Main competitors:** C-01 if the move is better read as ongoing continuation; C-04 if the release is failing; C-05 if range rejection dominates instead of discovery.
**Main anti-confusions:** print-through ≠ breakout, volatility burst ≠ pressure-resolution, late chase ≠ valid release.
**Typical pattern children:** G1 accepted break-and-hold, G3 compression release.
**Stage posture:** strongest from emergent through confirmed; weaker when late/exhausted unless genuine acceptance persists.
**Data-surface reminder:** extra session, macro, or cross-asset support can help, but core structure must still stand on native chart/market-state truth.

### C-04 — Failed Break / Trap Reversal
**Canonical idea:** a prior break or directional attempt mattered and is now losing authority through visible failure/reclaim hierarchy.
**Native habitat:** failed release, reclaim sequence, trap unwind, reversal of prior directional authority.
**Anti-habitat:** unresolved drift that has not actually failed yet, ordinary pullback inside valid continuation, or noisy chop with no reclaim hierarchy.
**Main competitors:** C-03 while release still holds; C-02 when retracement remains constructive; C-05 when the case is really balance rejection rather than failed directional attempt.
**Main anti-confusions:** mere hesitation ≠ failure, rotation ≠ trap reversal, unstable chop ≠ reclaim structure.
**Typical pattern children:** G4 failed break/reclaim.
**Stage posture:** strong in developing/confirmed failure, sometimes salvage/re-entry, weak when still emergent or already exhausted.
**Data-surface reminder:** real failure becomes stronger when execution, regime, or participation surfaces confirm loss of prior authority, but Aurora must not invent those supports.

### C-05 — Balance Rotation / Range Mean-Reversion
**Canonical idea:** accepted value/balance dominates and edge excursion rejects back toward value rather than discovering new directional migration.
**Native habitat:** real balance, repeatable range edges, and rejection/return behavior.
**Anti-habitat:** clean one-way migration, authentic release/acceptance outside balance, or unstable pseudo-range structure.
**Main competitors:** C-03 when excursion is becoming accepted breakout; C-04 when the move is a failed break hierarchy rather than ordinary range rejection; C-01/C-02 when directional authority dominates over rotation.
**Main anti-confusions:** every sideways patch is not balance, every reversal near an edge is not mean reversion, and late drift inside value is not the same as fresh rejection.
**Typical pattern children:** G5 range rejection/return to value.
**Stage posture:** strongest when balance is already confirmed; weaker when balance is only assumed or when migration authority is taking over.
**Bucket/regime note:** range logic is especially vulnerable to regime shifts and false “cheap mean-reversion” assumptions.

## Multi-candidate preservation law

Do not erase plausible competitors too early.
Examples:
- a C-03 release may need C-04 to stay live until acceptance is proven
- a C-02 restoration may need C-05 preserved if balance is reforming
- a C-05 rejection may need C-03 nearby when edge acceptance is unresolved

## Anti-confusion and rejection law

When rejecting a family, preserve why:
- anti-habitat conflict too large
- contradiction load too large
- dependence on missing surfaces too high
- stronger competitor explains the structure better
- required evidence is absent in a load-bearing way

Never reject with vague “low confidence” wording alone.
