# 1. ROOT LAW

Aurora hostile-environment doctrine exists to prevent clean-looking local opportunities from being mistaken for deployable opportunities when the surrounding environment is degrading payoff quality, execution reliability, or risk asymmetry.

Wave 4 is not allowed to become:
- generic fear language
- volatility worship
- one-number risk scoring without context
- macro storytelling detached from local structures
- local chart optimism that ignores hostile context

A valid Wave 4 reading must ask:
- what type of hostility is present?
- where is the hostility located?
- does it invalidate structure, degrade deployability, or only reduce confidence?
- what parts of the environment are local and what parts are backdrop-wide?

---

# 2. WHAT WAVE 4 IS FOR

Wave 4 exists to make Aurora:
- realism-preserving under stress
- aware of asymmetry deterioration
- aware of volatility and fragility context
- aware of execution degradation
- aware of macro pressure and order-shift backdrop
- less naive about the difference between visible opportunity and survivable opportunity

It is the bridge between:
- Wave 1 structure and execution surface
- Wave 3 research/evaluation discipline
- Wave 5 review/adaptation under pressure

If Wave 4 is weak, the system will over-trust local structure and under-price hostile context.

# 3. CANONICAL HOSTILITY OBJECTS

Every serious hostility assessment should preserve these objects explicitly.

## 3.1 Hostility type
What kind of hostility is present?
Examples:
- volatility expansion / nonlinear instability
- execution friction / thinness / spread burden
- policy distortion
- debt-cycle or order-transition pressure
- tail-risk sensitivity
- cross-asset backdrop strain

## 3.2 Location of hostility
Where is the problem located?
Examples:
- inside local structure
- inside execution surface
- inside broader regime
- inside macro backdrop
- inside portfolio interaction or correlation behavior

## 3.3 Effect class
What does the hostility do?
Examples:
- structural invalidation
- deployability degradation
- expected-return compression
- confidence reduction
- tail-risk amplification
- unusual transmission or follow-through failure

## 3.4 Persistence / stage
Is the hostility:
- temporary
- recurring
- building
- late-stage and acute
- distorted but not yet collapsing?

## 3.5 Response boundary
What should Aurora do with this hostility?
Examples:
- suppress deployment
- reduce confidence
- demand stronger evidence
- require lower friction or stronger asymmetry
- reclassify the environment as nonstandard

# 4. HOSTILITY LAWS

## Law 1 — Hostility is not one thing
Wave 4 should preserve multiple hostility classes rather than collapsing them into one risk label.

## Law 2 — Local cleanliness does not defeat backdrop fragility
A clean local setup can still sit inside a hostile environment that degrades payoff quality or reliability.

## Law 3 — Structural invalidity and deployability loss are different
Some hostile conditions make the setup wrong.
Others leave the setup logically intact but practically unattractive.
Aurora must keep those separate.

## Law 4 — Earlier warning matters
Hostility does not always appear as obvious collapse.
It can appear as distortion, degraded transmission, or unstable background pressure before breakdown becomes visible.

## Law 5 — Macro hostility is real but bounded
Macro debt-cycle, political, and order-shift stress should be preserved as a valid hostility class, but it must not be used as an excuse to overwrite local structure without evidence.

## Law 6 — Cross-asset context matters
Premia shifts and backdrop strain across major asset classes can change the opportunity environment.
This is a context layer, not a replacement for the traded market’s actual structure.

## Law 7 — Hostility should change evidence standards
In more hostile environments, Aurora should demand stronger evidence, cleaner asymmetry, or lower implementation burden.

# 5. CANONICAL HOSTILITY WORKFLOW

## Step 1 — Identify hostility type
Name what kind of hostility is present.
Do not jump immediately from “stress exists” to “do nothing.”

## Step 2 — Locate the hostility
Ask whether the problem sits in:
- local market structure
- execution surface
- volatility regime
- macro backdrop
- cross-asset environment

## Step 3 — Classify effect
Ask whether the hostility causes:
- invalidation
- degradation
- suppression of follow-through
- cost burden
- tail-risk amplification
- context uncertainty only

## Step 4 — Assess persistence and stage
Ask whether the condition is:
- brief
- cyclical
- escalating
- regime-defining
- late-stage and fragile

## Step 5 — Set response boundary
Wave 4 should not only diagnose.
It should define the boundary:
- what is disallowed?
- what is weakened?
- what requires stronger evidence?
- what belongs in “observe only” status?

# 6. VOLATILITY / NONLINEARITY DOCTRINE

## 6.1 Volatility is not just bigger movement
Volatility matters because it changes:
- payoff shape
- follow-through reliability
- asymmetry quality
- tail-risk exposure
- correlation behavior

## 6.2 Nonlinearity matters
A hostile environment can create outcomes that are disproportionate to normal expectations.
Research and execution assumptions built in calm periods may become fragile here.

## 6.3 Tail awareness is mandatory
Wave 4 should preserve the rule that rare but severe outcomes matter disproportionately in hostile environments.

---

# 7. EXECUTION HOSTILITY DOCTRINE

## 7.1 Tradability and visible opportunity are different
A setup may remain visible on chart while tradability deteriorates through:
- spreads
- thinness
- slippage
- delayed execution
- unstable participation depth

## 7.2 Execution hostility can erase edge without invalidating structure
Aurora must keep this separate from structural failure.
This is one of the main reasons Wave 4 must remain distinct from Wave 1.

## 7.3 Deployability loss is a first-class object
Research and wrapper logic should preserve deployability loss explicitly rather than hiding it inside vague risk language.

---

# 8. MACRO / ORDER-SHIFT DOCTRINE

## 8.1 Hostility can come from the macro backdrop
Debt-cycle stress, policy distortion, political instability, and order-transition pressure can create a hostile context even when local chart structure still looks coherent.

## 8.2 Backdrop fragility can compress expected return
Macro hostility can change:
- reliability of follow-through
- correlation behavior
- liquidity transmission
- confidence in normal payoff expectations

## 8.3 Macro hostility must remain bounded
Macro context should be used to pressure-test local opportunities, not to override them lazily.
Wave 4 should ask for evidence of actual degradation.

---

# 9. CROSS-ASSET PRESSURE DOCTRINE

## 9.1 Opportunity context is cross-asset-sensitive
Shifts in equity, bond, and credit premia can signal backdrop strain, opportunity competition, or broader regime pressure.

## 9.2 Cross-asset context is advisory, not sovereign
It informs the local reading.
It does not erase the local reading automatically.

## 9.3 Portfolio interaction matters
Hostility can grow through portfolio crowding, correlation shifts, or concentration of risk across seemingly separate ideas.

---

# 10. WAVE 4 RESPONSE DOCTRINE

When hostility rises, Aurora should be able to distinguish between:
- invalidation
- suppression
- stricter evidence requirement
- narrower deployment windows
- downgraded confidence
- nonstandard / observe-only status

Wave 4 should not default to permanent paralysis.
It should impose sharper boundaries.

---

# 11. WHAT WAVE 4 MUST NOT BECOME

Wave 4 must not become:
- permanent caution culture
- an excuse to avoid hard evaluation
- a macro-story override engine
- a vague “market is risky” ritual
- a place where everything hostile is treated as equally hostile

If Wave 4 loses discrimination, it stops protecting the system and starts starving it.
