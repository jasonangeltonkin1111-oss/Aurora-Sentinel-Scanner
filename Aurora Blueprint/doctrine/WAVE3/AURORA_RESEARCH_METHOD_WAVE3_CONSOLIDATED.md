# 1. ROOT LAW

Aurora research exists to reduce false confidence, increase discrimination, and improve survival of real edges.

Research is not allowed to become:
- narrative decoration
- backtest theater
- complexity worship
- rule dumping without context
- model-chasing detached from family logic or implementation reality

A valid Wave 3 research process must:
- define what edge is being claimed
- define where that claim should work
- define where that claim should fail
- define how it will be evaluated
- define what costs, frictions, or context may erase the apparent edge

# 3. CANONICAL RESEARCH OBJECTS

Every serious research item should preserve these objects explicitly.

## 3.1 Structural claim
What market behavior, family, pattern, or relationship is being claimed?

Examples:
- continuation after specific compression-release context
- reversion inside a certain range habitat
- relative-value restoration under spread dislocation

## 3.2 Habitat
Where should it work?

This includes:
- market-state context
- execution-surface context
- volatility/hostility context
- macro/context backdrop if relevant

## 3.3 Anti-habitat
Where should it weaken or fail?

This includes:
- structurally hostile conditions
- wrong regime or state
- poor execution surface
- macro stress or backdrop distortion when relevant

## 3.4 Expected return
What payoff is being claimed, and what supports that expectation?

Expected return is not a vague afterthought.
It should be treated as an explicit research object.

## 3.5 Implementation burden
What frictions, delays, costs, thinness, or deployment complications may erase the edge?

## 3.6 Evidence class
What kind of evidence currently supports the claim?
Aurora should keep these separate:
- observed on chart or tape
- inferred from framework
- quant hypothesis
- tested / not yet tested

# 4. RESEARCH LAWS

## Law 1 — Family before formula
Research should begin from family logic or meaningful structural claims, not from blind signal mining.

## Law 2 — Habitat before verdict
A method cannot be judged honestly without specifying where it is supposed to work.

## Law 3 — Anti-habitat is mandatory
A method without a failure map is not mature.

## Law 4 — Expected return must be explicit
Research should preserve expected return as an object of estimation, comparison, and decomposition.

## Law 5 — Evaluation must stay near implementation
Signal quality alone is not enough. Trading costs, slippage, friction, portfolio consequence, and deployability matter during evaluation.

## Law 6 — Complexity must earn admission
Higher model complexity is allowed only when it adds robust leverage, wider useful information capture, or materially better decision support.

## Law 7 — ML remains subordinate to structure
Machine learning is a research extension, not a replacement for family logic, economic structure, or evidence discipline.

## Law 8 — Review must protect against false certainty
Research must resist hindsight contamination, narrative rescue, overconfidence, and story-driven conviction.

# 5. CANONICAL RESEARCH WORKFLOW

## Step 1 — Define the claim
State clearly:
- what the setup/family/model claims
- what event or relationship is expected
- what edge mechanism is believed to exist

## Step 2 — Define habitat and anti-habitat
Name:
- where it should work
- where it should fail
- what context variables matter most

## Step 3 — Define measurable translation
Research should ask:
- what variables can express the claim?
- what proxies are needed?
- what measurable conditions represent entry, exit, suppression, and failure?

## Step 4 — Define expected-return logic
Research should ask:
- what payoff is expected?
- what decomposition supports the expectation?
- how time-varying is the opportunity?
- what broader premia or cross-asset context matters?

## Step 5 — Define evaluation path
Evaluation should include:
- simple baseline comparisons
- out-of-sample checks
- regime/context segmentation
- anti-overfitting checks
- negative-evidence review

## Step 6 — Define implementation reality
Evaluation must remain connected to:
- slippage
- spread burden
- participation depth
- delays
- trading costs
- deployability loss
- portfolio/ranking consequences

## Step 7 — Define review feedback loop
After evaluation or live observation, research should ask:
- did the idea fail structurally or contextually?
- did the environment change?
- did execution burden erase the edge?
- did operator bias or physiology distort the reading?

# 6. EXPECTED RETURN DOCTRINE

## 6.1 Expected return is explicit, not implied
Aurora research should treat expected return as a first-class object.

Research should ask:
- what return is being expected?
- from what mechanism?
- under what context?
- against what baseline?

## 6.2 Expected return can be decomposed
Expected return may come from:
- structural edge
- volatility/risk context
- carry or premia context
- relative attractiveness across assets
- temporary dislocation or compression

## 6.3 Expected return is time-varying
Research should avoid assuming static edge quality.
Context, regime, and broader asset-class pressure can alter opportunity quality materially.

## 6.4 Cross-asset context matters
Broader equity, bond, and credit premia conditions can shape the background quality or competition of opportunities.
This should remain a context layer, not an excuse to override the local setup without evidence.

---

# 7. EVIDENCE DOCTRINE

## 7.1 Positive evidence is not enough
A mature research item should preserve:
- positive evidence
- negative evidence
- anti-habitat evidence
- implementation-friction evidence

## 7.2 Baselines matter
A more complex method should be compared against simpler benchmarks.
If it does not beat them robustly, it has not earned complexity.

## 7.3 Out-of-sample leverage matters more than in-sample elegance
Research should prefer boring robustness over elegant overfit behavior.

## 7.4 Context segmentation matters
Results should be examined across:
- regimes
- states
- execution surfaces
- hostile environments
- macro pressure when relevant

---

# 8. MACHINE LEARNING DOCTRINE INSIDE WAVE 3

## 8.1 Why ML is allowed
ML is allowed because finance often contains:
- large information sets
- ambiguous functional forms
- interactions that simple rules may miss

## 8.2 Why ML is dangerous
ML can also create:
- overfitting
- detached prediction scores with no economic meaning
- fragile dependence on unstable patterns
- evaluation blindness to costs and implementation

## 8.3 Canonical ML rule
ML should remain a disciplined extension of research/testing, tied to:
- experimental design
- simpler baselines
- economic/strategy structure
- implementation consequences

## 8.4 Prediction is not the end state
Research does not end at return prediction.
It must remain connected to:
- portfolio choice
- ranking and weighting
- trading costs
- suppression under fragile conditions

# 9. REVIEW DOCTRINE INSIDE WAVE 3

Wave 3 research must remain review-aware.

Research should preserve checks against:
- hindsight contamination
- illusion of validity
- coherent-story rescue after the fact
- house-money distortion
- break-even escalation
- overconfidence after success
- fear contraction after losses

Review should ask whether failure came from:
- bad structure
- wrong habitat
- degraded execution surface
- hostile environment
- changing ecology
- operator bias or physiology
